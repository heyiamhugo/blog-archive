5 must-have items every designer needs in their toolkit

  • Hugo
  • October 28, 2023
  • Design, Tips

During my time in college and the beginning of my career, Ive found out a lot of stuff that isnt really that useful for my work and a lot of stuff that is, with this in mind today I want to share with you 5 items that are must-haves for every designers toolkit.

Photo by Theme Photos on Unsplash

First of all, some disclaimers. Im not going to suggest stuff that is very basic like a laptop, there are already a bunch of articles like this, I wanted to instead suggest useful items that go beyond the very basic of what you need. Another quick disclaimer, some links here are going to be affiliate links, no cost for you but it helps me out if you buy stuff through the link.

If you are completely new, and you truly were searching for a very basic article of what you needed, here is a very quick list of stuff that hopefully will help you, so you dont spend your time reading 1000 articles.

Although you may not need all of this it is going to cover 99% of your needs when starting. The most important thing is to not be a gear nerd, start with what you have and build it up, anyways here are some suggestions for essentials:

  1. Laptop
  2. Adobe software and if appliable Figma
  3. Graphic diary
  4. USB drive &/or External hard drive
  5. Metal ruler, X-acto Knife & Cutting Mat
  6. Tracing Paper, normal paper & Cardstock of different colors
  7. Pencils of different strengths & Pens
  8. Printer & Scanner
  9. Good books (Im going to write an article on design book suggestions next week, stay tuned)
  10. In-ear monitors & Good Music

Some stuff I haven’t linked because it either depends on preference or just overall knowledge I have with said product, for example I really like the Staedtler Norris line, specifically the B1 pencil and I have no knowledge on what the best printers are although I like Epson. With the basics removed out of the way here are the another 5 must-have items that every designer needs in their toolkit.

Pantone Formula Guides

Its funny that I just wrote this article on Pantone and the first thing Im suggesting is their Formula Guide sets. They are extremely useful for a variety of applications and a variety of designers. Its a color system that has been refined and standardized over decades and it can now be in the palm of your hands (literally).

I used them at university for a bunch of different projects and I use them now on basically a day-to-day basis in a plethora of different projects. They are an essential item every agency and studio owns and an item that should be on your acquirement list.

Dont be that designer who picks Pantone colors by looking at their monitors, please I beg you. Ive met a few people who have done work like this and it bothers me on a whole new level.

Personally, I have the standard non-bridge Formula Guide set which I bought before the whole Adobe and Pantone situation which led to the dreadful implementation of Pantone Connect. If you are acquiring your Pantone catalogs now, I recommend you to get the Pantone Formula Guide Bridge Coated & Uncoated set, so you dont have issues when picking colors for projects where you need CMYK and RGB values.

Also, a quick tip Pantone on the back of their Formula guide sets claim they last for only a year, if you preserve your guides in a dark environment with no humidity, they will last you years. One of my teachers showed us her guides with 40+ years of use, the colors were mostly perfect. Think about it.

CMY color wheel

This item was also recommended by a teacher and it took me years to find it online being sold again. Its a color wheel that helps you figure out color combinations, theres also an app that I would recommend for Android that I used previously. To be honest, if you can buy a physical version of the wheel, its cheap and feels better than the one on your phone.

This color wheel is very well printed with a fair amount of accuracy and attention to detail which is also important. Using a color wheel is in my opinion an essential part of my workflow and an inexpensive incredibly useful tool.

Also, if you dont want to get the one from the link I provided, make sure you get a CMY color wheel, dont get a RYB color wheel.

The RYB color wheel was created in the early days of color theory when we did not know about the existence of pigments Cyan and Magenta which are nowadays the standard for printing.

The RYB color wheel unfortunately is still taught in a lot of schools and even universities, even a lot of design books still teach that model and subsequently there are a lot of wheels around that follow the RYB model, while its great for painting its awful for design, just remember that.

Basically, make sure you buy a CMY color wheel which is much more useful and updated. Also, a huge thank you to Professor Dulce for teaching this and explaining it in detail, happy to have learned from you.

Ergonomic Chair

Dont be like Spiderman get a good quality chair. Im going to be honest with you here I dont have any specific recommendation to give you since everybody is different, but there are a few tips I can give you:

  • Forget about gaming chairs most of them are offensively bad for your posture, racing chairs are for racing not for you to be looking at a monitor 8h per day.
  • If its very cheap the materials are probably not that great, consider getting fewer features to save money instead of losing on material quality.
  • I really like this video comparing chairs, I think you should watch it.

Every person is different but maintaining a good posture is a universal thing, I also recommend you to do a lot of breaks and frequent exercise. There is no chair on the market that will fix any of these issues for you.

Good mouse and keyboard

A good mouse and keyboard are essential tools to us who actually properly use them for hours per day, but beware the industry is full of bad products.

What I can recommend from experience is the Logitech k120 keyboard which I used for years, the model O, the G203 Prodigy mouse, and the Mx Master 3.

The K120 keyboard is cheap, works great, and in my opinion is an absolute steal, out of the mice I recommended all of them are also equally great but the Mx Master 3 is by far my favorite mouse ever, so much that Im even getting another one to use in my internship.

Also, I recently bought the Mx Master 3 and Mx Keys combo which Im going to review on the blog, stay tuned.

Paper catalogs

Last but not least on my list are paper catalogs. I absolutely dont understand why people dont talk about these more often they are essential tools for anyone working with any type of physical stuff they need to design. Business cards, books, magazines, and newspapers all need one thing paper!

So, a catalog is by far one of the most useful tools you can have around.

My recommendation is to first check local print manufacturers, in Portugal, we have Inapa, for example. These are probably the most common types of papers that your printers provide. Beyond this I would recommend two paper manufacturer catalogs Metapapers Sample Book and Fedrigonis Paper Box.

Out of these two Fedrigonis paper box is the most complete although it is a paid product and unfortunately currently is slightly outdated since they have removed some products.

I have a nice customer experience with them and you can request any paper samples you want from their website for free! They are very considerate!

On a final note I wanted to just tell you that tools are just tools, without the knowledge you can have the best laptop and not be an amazing designer, skills come first dont be a gearhead.
